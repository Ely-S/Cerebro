Cerebral Oximeter

Patient Driven Science — Single-Channel NIRS Cerebral Oxygenation Monitor

Patient Driven Science POTS / Orthostatic Intolerance Open Hardware Rev 3.2

About This Project

This device was built by patients, for patients. POTS (Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome) and orthostatic intolerance are conditions that are often invisible to outside observers and difficult to demonstrate in clinical or educational settings. This project puts measurement tools directly in the hands of the people who need them most.

Using near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS), the device detects real-time relative changes in cerebral oxygenation through the forehead. Two LEDs (730 nm and 850 nm) illuminate tissue while a BPW34 photodiode captures returned light, producing a visible oxygenation trend during postural changes — the kind of drop POTS patients experience every time they stand up.

Built from off-the-shelf components for under $100, this is an open hardware platform anyone can build, verify, and use to document their own physiology. Signal chain: photodiode → MCP6022 TIA (100 kΩ) → stage 2 gain (48 V/V) → ADS1115 ADC (64 SPS) → Arduino Nano 33 IoT with 4-state TDM and EMA filtering.

Why Patient Driven Science Matters

Many chronic conditions — POTS, Long COVID, ME/CFS, and other dysautonomia syndromes — are poorly understood, frequently dismissed, and chronically underfunded. Patients often wait years for a diagnosis and longer still for research that reflects their lived experience. Traditional medical research moves slowly and is shaped by institutional priorities that don't always align with what patients actually need to know.

Patient driven science changes that. When patients build their own measurement tools, they can document their own physiology on their own timeline, generate data that is grounded in real symptoms and real conditions, and contribute evidence that the medical system has not yet produced. This kind of self-directed inquiry has a long history of accelerating understanding — from the HIV/AIDS activists who reshaped clinical trial design in the 1980s to the patient communities today mapping Long COVID symptoms faster than any single research institution.

Open hardware tools lower the barrier to participation. A device that costs under $100 and can be built and verified by an engineering student — or a patient with basic electronics exposure — makes measurement accessible outside of hospitals and labs. Every person who builds this device and records their own data is doing science that matters.

For ME/CFS and Long COVID

Conventional orthostatic testing looks for two things: a drop in blood pressure (hypotension) or a spike in heart rate (tachycardia). For many people with POTS, that is enough. But for a large portion of ME/CFS and Long COVID patients, heart rate and blood pressure can look completely normal during a tilt test — while cerebral blood flow is collapsing.

A 2020 controlled study by van Campen et al. measured cerebral blood flow in 429 ME/CFS patients during head-up tilt testing. 90% showed abnormal cerebral blood flow reduction exceeding 13%, compared to 7% in healthy controls. Critically, 82% of those patients had normal heart rate and blood pressure responses — meaning standard vital sign monitoring would have missed the problem entirely.

This is why direct cerebral oxygenation measurement matters. A device that tracks NIRS signals at the forehead can surface circulatory dysfunction that HR and BP monitoring cannot see. For patients whose symptoms are dismissed because their vitals "look fine," this kind of independent signal is not just useful — it may be the only objective evidence available.

van Campen, C.M.C., Verheugt, F.W.A., Rowe, P.C., & Visser, F.C. (2020). Cerebral blood flow is reduced in ME/CFS during head-up tilt testing even in the absence of hypotension or tachycardia: A quantitative, controlled study using Doppler echography. Clinical Neurophysiology Practice, 5, 50–58. PMC7044650
